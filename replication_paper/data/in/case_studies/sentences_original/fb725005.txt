In a secret codicil, Kennedy agreed to quietly withdraw United States nuclear-armed Thor and Jupiter missiles targeted on the USSR from Turkey and Italy.
Washington hailed it as a huge victory for President Kennedy, who became a national hero and icon.
To prevent those missiles from being used, America could have ordered an amphibious invasion, an airborne assault, and an overland push from Guantanamo Bay, Cuba.
Fifty years ago, in October 1962, the world teetered on the brink of nuclear war.
It was after days of an agonisingly tense standoff that negotiations eventually prevailed.
Similar to responses to other foreign crises both before and since, the Cuban missile crisis drew the country together as people rallied around the president.
